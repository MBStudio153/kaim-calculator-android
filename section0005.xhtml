Appendix 2: Non-Tech Large Caps KAIM Analysis — FY2025

Seven large-cap companies from healthcare, financials, retail, energy, and consumer staples.

 

Forward Asset Methodology Note: None of the seven non-tech companies analyzed below disclose a forward purchase commitment category comparable to a tech company's chip backlog or cloud service agreement. JPMorgan Chase's commitments footnote, for example, is dominated by lending-related commitments — an entirely different category serving an entirely different economic function, and not a forward knowledge-factory asset in the KAIM sense. KAIM Book Value for all seven companies below reflects GAAP equity plus Knowledge Capital Stock only, with no forward asset layer added. As technology and AI infrastructure investment becomes more material to non-tech sectors, future 10-K disclosures may begin to support a comparable forward asset calculation — this remains an open area for the model to extend into as company reporting evolves.

 

 

Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) | Healthcare / Pharma | FY End: Dec 28, 2025

What Was Analyzed

Johnson & Johnson was analyzed as the premier test case for KAIM outside tech. Pharmaceutical R&D is perhaps the purest analog to the knowledge factory model: a drug candidate takes 10–15 years from discovery to approval, the investment is expensed immediately under GAAP, and the resulting patent and clinical expertise creates durable competitive advantages. The 20-year KCS includes Darzalex, Stelara, Carvykti CAR-T therapy, and the Janssen oncology pipeline. KAIM reveals J&J's balance sheet understates its productive asset base by more than 2x.

Data note: Heavy R&D pharma company. KCS reflects decades of drug/device pipeline investment.

Cross-Sector Findings

The non-tech comparison confirms two things simultaneously. First, KAIM is not universally applicable in the same way across all sectors. Berkshire Hathaway carries almost no R&D — its value comes from capital allocation, not knowledge production — and GAAP actually serves it well. ExxonMobil's R&D is modest relative to its physical asset base, and the KAIM correction is correspondingly small.

Second, the model reveals genuinely hidden value in knowledge-intensive non-tech companies. Johnson & Johnson's pharmaceutical pipeline represents decades of compounding R&D investment that GAAP expenses immediately — KAIM reveals a book value roughly 2–3x the GAAP figure. UnitedHealth's Optum technology platform carries a similar hidden asset.

The common thread: wherever R&D produces durable competitive advantages that outlast their GAAP amortization period, KAIM will find hidden value. What changes across sectors is the size of the knowledge factory relative to the physical capital already on the balance sheet.

 

Sector Takeaways

 

Healthcare / Pharma (JNJ)  ~4x GAAP book

Drug pipelines are pure knowledge factories. 10-15yr R&D cycles fully invisible under GAAP. KAIM correction is largest in non-tech universe.

Healthcare / Insurance (UNH)  ~1.6x GAAP book

Optum's data analytics platform is a durable knowledge asset. Tech investment proxied from disclosures rather than explicit R&D line.

Consumer Staples (PG)  ~2x GAAP book

Formulation R&D (enzyme chemistry, blade metallurgy) accumulates over decades. Hybrid physical/knowledge factory.

Financial Services (JPM)  ~1.3x GAAP book

Tech investment classified as operating expense, not R&D. KAIM correction modest but growing rapidly as AI spending accelerates.

Retail (WMT)  ~1.3x GAAP book

Primarily physical capital company. R&D trajectory accelerating — gap will widen materially over the next decade.

Energy (XOM)  ~1.1x GAAP book

GAAP is relatively informative here. Productive value lies in physical reserves, not knowledge assets. KAIM correctly produces minimal correction.

Conglomerate (BRK.B)  ~1.0x GAAP book

The control group. Near-zero R&D means GAAP P/B ≈ KAIM P/B. Model works as designed: no correction where no knowledge factory exists.
